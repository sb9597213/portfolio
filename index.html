Artifact 02 — Workshop 2 — Assignment 2.3

ML vs. Deep Learning — A Business Analyst's Real-World Comparison

Written Analysis

A comparative analysis of machine learning and deep learning through two industry-defining real-world applications — Gmail's spam filter (Logistic Regression) and Google Translate (Transformer models). Making the technical distinction immediately relevant to practical business decision-making.

Objective

To demonstrate the ability to differentiate between ML and DL approaches by grounding the comparison in real business applications that technology leaders and product managers recognize.

Description

Gmail uses Logistic Regression for spam detection where known features and interpretability needs make traditional ML ideal. Google Translate uses Transformer models where the contextual complexity of language translation across 100+ languages makes deep learning the only viable approach.

Case Studies

ML — Gmail Spam (Logistic Regression)

Binary classification with well-understood features. Fast, scalable, interpretable. Google can explain why an email was flagged — critical for user trust and compliance (Amazon, n.d.).

DL — Google Translate (Transformer)

Language translation requires understanding context and nuance across 100+ language pairs. Transformer models learn automatically through self-attention mechanisms — impossible to manually engineer (Illing, 2024).

Artifact 03 — Workshop 3 — Discussion 3.4

Neural Networks Explained Simply — AI-Assisted Communication for Learning

Communication for Learning

A carefully crafted simple explanation of neural networks in machine learning — developed in collaboration with Claude AI and grounded in communication science principles including chunking, storytelling, progressive disclosure, and multimodal presentation. Demonstrates the ability to make complex AI concepts accessible to non-technical business audiences.

Objective

To apply communication for learning strategies — drawn from change leadership and information presentation theory — to explain a complex AI concept (neural networks) in the simplest possible terms, using AI as a collaborative tool in the communication design process.

Description

Using Einstein's principle — "if you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough" — this artifact produces a layered, analogy-driven explanation of neural networks that a non-technical business professional can understand and remember. The explanation was developed iteratively with Claude AI and refined through multiple drafts.

The Simple Explanation — Neural Networks

🧠 Imagine you are training a new employee.

On their first day, you give them a stack of customer emails and say: "Sort these into two piles — complaints and compliments."

They guess. They get some right, some wrong. You correct them. They adjust. They try again. They get a little better. You do this hundreds of times. Eventually — without you ever writing down a rule — they just know. They have learned the patterns from experience.

That is a neural network.

It is not programmed with rules. It is trained with examples. It makes guesses, gets corrected, and adjusts — over and over — until it gets reliably good at recognizing patterns.

The "neurons" are tiny decision-makers arranged in layers. Each one takes information in, does a small calculation, and passes a signal forward. The layers work together — the first might notice individual words, the next notices phrases, the next notices tone. Together they figure out: complaint or compliment?

Communication Strategies Applied

Chunking

Broke the concept into three digestible moments: the analogy, the correction loop, and the layer explanation. No cognitive overload.

Storytelling

The new employee scenario makes an abstract technical concept feel human and familiar — giving the reader a mental hook they can hold onto.

Progressive Disclosure

Started with the simplest version (training with examples) before introducing the layer structure. Foundation first, complexity second.

Relevance & Context

Connected neural networks to something everyone understands — learning from feedback. That is the "why it makes sense" moment that makes new information stick.

Multimodal Thinking

Used emoji, bold formatting, and italic emphasis to create visual breaks and guide the reader's eye rather than presenting a wall of text.

AI Collaboration

Used Claude to test clarity, identify hidden technical assumptions, and refine analogy selection across multiple drafts before finalizing.

AI-Assisted Process

1

Initial Draft: Wrote a first explanation of neural networks — too technical, too many assumptions about prior knowledge.

2

Claude Collaboration: Asked Claude to identify where the explanation was still assuming knowledge the reader might not have. Claude flagged terms like "parameters" and "weights" that needed either removal or analogy replacement.

3

Analogy Testing: Tested three different analogies — recipe following, animal training, and new employee training. The new employee scenario scored highest on relatability for a business professional audience.

4

Final Refinement: Stripped out every remaining technical term that was not essential. The final version is shorter and clearer than anything I would have written alone.
